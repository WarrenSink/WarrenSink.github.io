Identical

2019-12-01 — Written by Warren Sink with the image from Martin Schoeller's book, Identical.

Dr. Ross Geller : You know what I think?… She's weird. It's because she's a twin.

Rachel Green : Ross, she is not weird. She just likes her stuff to be one-of-a-kind.

Dr. Ross Geller : You know what's not one-of-a-kind? A twin!

-Friends (Ep: The One with the Apothecary Table)

Fortunately, we don't live in a world described by Ross where identical twins are absolutely identical.

“Identical twins” in precise bio-jargon are monozygotic individuals - one sperm fertilizes one egg, and the subsequent embryo forms two separate individuals with (nearly) identical genomes. Nonetheless, if you are familiar with a pair of identical twins, it becomes quickly obvious: despite their similar looks and mannerisms, they are their own. A quick google search for the differences biologically of identical twins will lead you to cases of “discordancy” w.r.t. disease and aging, which goes to show that of course they don't identical lives. The divergences lead to another, broader question: what other discordances are there, which exist non-threateningly, resulting from the slightly stochastic nature of development? I'm currently working on a project in the Pospisilik lab at VAI to determine if there is such evidence in the faces of identical twins photographed by Martin Schoeller and inserted into his book, “Identical”.
